Since you said "for fun, non-technical", I'd lean into books that make you fall in love with aviation rather than teach you regulations. These are repeatedly recommended by pilots, aviation organizations, and aviation communities.

🥇 Tier 1 – Read these first

1. Fate Is the Hunter — Ernest K. Gann

⭐ Often described as the greatest aviation memoir ever written.

A commercial pilot recounts the early days of airline flying, where weather, navigation, and judgment could mean the difference between life and death. AOPA pilots consistently rank it among the greatest aviation books. 


---

2. West with the Night — Beryl Markham

One of the most beautifully written books about flying.

It's about adventure, courage, and pioneering aviation in Africa—especially meaningful given your location. A favorite recommendation from AOPA readers. 


---

3. Skyfaring — Mark Vanhoenacker

Written by a long-haul airline captain.

Instead of focusing on technical flying, it explores what it feels like to fly across oceans, above storms, and through the night. Many pilots recommend it to people dreaming of airline careers. 


---

4. Flight of Passage — Rinker Buck

Two brothers fly a tiny vintage airplane across America.

It's adventurous, funny, and reminds you that aviation is also about freedom. Frequently recommended by AOPA. 


---

5. The Wright Brothers — David McCullough

The story of how Wilbur and Orville Wright changed history.

Less about airplanes and more about persistence, curiosity, and solving impossible problems. 


---

🥈 Tier 2 – Aviation stories you'll remember

Skunk Works — Ben R. Rich

The story behind the SR-71 Blackbird, F-117 stealth fighter, and Lockheed's secret aircraft programs. A favorite among pilots and aerospace enthusiasts. 


QF32 — Richard de Crespigny

The captain's firsthand account of safely landing an Airbus A380 after a catastrophic engine failure.


Yeager — Chuck Yeager

The autobiography of the legendary test pilot who first broke the sound barrier.


Chickenhawk — Robert Mason

A gripping memoir of flying helicopters during the Vietnam War.




---

🥉 Books that make you think like a pilot

The Killing Zone — Paul Craig

Explores why many accidents happen during a pilot's early flying career.


Yikes! — Jeffrey James Madison

One hundred real pilot mistakes and the lessons learned from them.

A thought about reading

You've already chosen books that sharpen your cybersecurity mindset. Pairing them with aviation memoirs gives you something different: they don't just teach you how airplanes fly—they show you how pilots think, how they make decisions, recover from mistakes, and carry responsibility.

That combination—technical curiosity on one shelf and aviation stories on the other—fits the journey you're building toward.
